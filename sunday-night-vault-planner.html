Where the number nine comes from

Your two courses are three credits each. The college tells you to plan for nine hours a week per course. That number is not a guess somebody made up, and it is not a threat. It comes from an accounting problem in 1906.

Andrew Carnegie wanted to fund pensions for college professors. To hand out the money, the Carnegie Foundation needed a rule for what counted as a college in the first place, and the thing they could actually measure was time in a classroom. The Carnegie Unit came out of that, and the credit hour grew out of the Carnegie Unit.

The definition in use today sets one credit hour at roughly one hour of instruction plus a minimum of two hours of your own work outside of class, every week, across a fifteen week semester. Three credits times three hours is nine. Two courses is eighteen.

The part that catches working students An asynchronous online course has no seat time, and the expectation does not shrink to match. Many adults read “online” as “faster.” It is not. The classroom hour did not disappear, it moved into the lecture videos and the reading, and it now has to fit inside the same evening as your job and your family.

One honest note before you go on. The credit hour measures time, not learning. It cannot tell whether the reading landed. So your real numbers will not match the standard exactly, and neither do ours. That gap is the next thing you need to see.

The gap is not padding and it is not the college being cautious. It is the reread when the chapter did not land. It is the source that turned out to be useless. It is the video that would not load and the discussion partner who posted late. Students who schedule 16 hours have no slack, so the first thing that goes wrong costs them the week. Students who schedule 18 absorb it. In a minute you will place a two hour buffer block, and now you know what it is for.
